When Anxiety
Takes the Wheel

Understanding why your anxiety shows up the way it does after trauma — and learning to work with it, rather than being dragged behind it.

Anxiety after sexual assault is not a personality flaw or a sign that you are falling apart. It is a nervous system that learned the world is not always safe — and is trying, imperfectly and loudly, to keep you from getting hurt again. You don't have to live at its mercy forever.

📚 Let's Learn

What Trauma Does to Anxiety

Anxiety is not a symptom that appears in isolation after sexual assault. It is often the surface expression of a nervous system that has fundamentally reorganized itself around the expectation of threat. When your brain and body learned — through direct, violating experience — that danger can come without warning from people who are supposed to be safe, anxiety becomes a logical response to a world that actually did prove itself unsafe.

How Trauma-Related Anxiety Differs

Hypervigilance

A state of heightened alertness — constantly scanning for danger. You may monitor people's moods, exits in rooms, sounds, facial expressions. It is exhausting and often invisible to others.

Intrusive Thoughts

Unwanted, distressing memories, images, or thoughts that arrive without invitation. Not a sign of obsession or weakness. A hallmark of how trauma interrupts normal thought processing.

Anticipatory Anxiety

The dread of something bad happening before it does — staying in a constant state of waiting for the next threat. Often leads to avoidance of people, places, or activities connected to the trauma.

Somatic Anxiety

Anxiety that lives in the body — nausea, chest tightness, shortness of breath, tingling, dizziness. Your body is expressing what your mind may not yet have words for.

The anxiety-threat connection: Dr. Judith Herman's foundational work on trauma established that hyperarousal and hypervigilance are not character weaknesses — they are the predictable neurological aftereffects of overwhelming threat. Your brain was rewired to protect you. The problem is that it didn't get the memo that the threat is over.

Triggers: When the Past Lives in the Present

A trigger is any sensory, emotional, or contextual cue that your nervous system has associated with the traumatic event. When a trigger is encountered, the brain responds as if the original threat is happening again — flooding the body with stress hormones, activating survival responses, and sometimes producing vivid sensory re-experiencing (flashbacks). This is not "going crazy." It is the memory system confusing then and now.

Triggers are often subtle and unexpected — a song, a scent, a phrase someone uses, a specific time of year, a texture, a certain quality of light. They are deeply individual, and they are not always logical. That is part of what makes them so disorienting.

For Black women navigating assault: Triggers can be compounded by racial dynamics. Encounters with institutions — police, hospitals, courts — that have historically been unsafe for Black women can trigger the original trauma even when seeking help or safety. The relationship between racial trauma and sexual trauma is not additive. It is intersectional — each amplifying the other in ways that deserve to be named and held.

Understanding Your Anxiety Patterns

Mapping your anxiety is not about being clinical with yourself. It's about building self-knowledge — and that is power.

Prompt 01

What does your anxiety feel like in your body — specifically? Not just "anxious" or "stressed," but the actual physical sensations. Where does it live? What does it do?

Prompt 02 — Trigger Mapping

Use this table to track any triggers you've noticed. Be as specific as you can — even things that feel "silly" or "shouldn't" bother you are worth noting.

What triggered it? Where were you? What did you feel in your body? What helped (or didn't)?
Prompt 03

Is there something — a place, a relationship, an activity — you've been avoiding because of anxiety since the assault? What would it mean to you if you could be in that space or do that thing again someday?

Prompt 04

If your anxiety could speak — if it had a voice and a reason — what do you think it would say it is trying to protect you from?

🌿 Ground Yourself

The TIPP Skill for Intense Anxiety

When anxiety spikes fast, you need something that works on the body — not the mind. The TIPP skill (from Dialectical Behavior Therapy) works by changing your physiological state directly, giving your nervous system a different input to respond to.

T

Temperature

Hold ice, splash cold water on your face, or dip your face in a bowl of cold water for 30 seconds. Cold activates the dive reflex, slowing your heart rate quickly and interrupting panic.

I

Intense Exercise

Run in place, do jumping jacks, or take a brisk walk for 1–2 minutes. Discharging the stress hormones through movement is one of the fastest ways to bring the body out of threat mode.

P

Paced Breathing

Inhale for 4 counts. Hold for 2. Exhale for 8. The extended exhale is key — it stimulates the vagus nerve and triggers your parasympathetic "rest" response.

P

Progressive Relaxation

Tense each muscle group for 5 seconds, then release. Start at your feet and move upward. The contrast between tension and release teaches your body the difference between stress and rest.

After using TIPP: Rate your anxiety from 0–10 before and after. Even a 1–2 point shift means your nervous system is responding. That is real, measurable progress. Write your numbers here:   Before: _____   After: _____
