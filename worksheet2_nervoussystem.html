Worksheet 02 · Nervous System & Survival

Your Nervous System
Is Working Hard

Learning to recognize your nervous system's responses — not as problems to be fixed, but as intelligent adaptations that helped you survive something that should never have happened to you.

You are not "too sensitive." You are not "overreacting." Your nervous system learned a set of lessons to protect you, and it is still applying them — even when you wish it would stop. That is not a failure. That is loyalty.

📚 Let's Learn

How Your Nervous System Responds to Threat

Your autonomic nervous system (ANS) regulates everything from your heart rate to your digestion — and it is always scanning the environment for safety or danger. This process, called neuroception, happens below conscious awareness. You don't decide to feel threatened. Your body decides first.

Neuroscientist Dr. Stephen Porges's Polyvagal Theory describes how the nervous system moves through distinct states depending on perceived safety. Understanding these states can help you recognize where you tend to land — and why.

The Four Survival Responses

🔥

Fight / Flight

Body cues:

Racing heart, tense muscles, shallow breathing, urge to run or push back. Hypervigilance. Difficulty sitting still or sleeping. May show up as anger, irritability, or feeling constantly on edge.

🧊

Freeze / Shutdown

Body cues:

Numbness, disconnection, feeling "not there," heaviness, inability to speak or move, emotional flatness. This is the oldest survival state — the nervous system's last resort when fight or flight isn't an option.

🕊️

Fawn

Body cues:

Compulsive people-pleasing, difficulty saying no, losing track of your own needs and feelings. An attempt to manage threat by making the threatening person comfortable. Common in trauma involving intimate relationships or repeated harm.

🌱

Safe & Social

Body cues:

Relaxed breath, present, able to connect, make eye contact, laugh, think clearly. This is your healing state. Trauma makes it harder to get here — but it is still available to you, even if it takes more work to find.

A note about fawn: Fawning is especially common among Black women navigating assault by someone known to them, assault in professional settings, or situations involving power imbalances. It is also culturally compounded — when you've been taught your entire life that your anger is dangerous, your safety is conditional, and your likability is a survival tool, fawning can become a default. This is not weakness. It is wisdom developed in survival conditions.

The Window of Tolerance

Developed by Dr. Daniel Siegel, the "window of tolerance" describes the zone where you can process thoughts and feelings without being overwhelmed by them. Sexual trauma often narrows this window significantly, making it easier to be flooded by emotion or to shut down entirely.

Hyperarousal Zone — Fight / Flight / Anxiety / Panic / Rage
↑ Too much activation: emotions feel out of control
Window of Tolerance — Present. Grounded. Able to feel and think at the same time.
← This is where healing happens →
Hypoarousal Zone — Freeze / Shutdown / Numbness / Dissociation
↓ Too little activation: disconnected from body and feelings

Trauma treatment aims to gradually widen your window — so more life fits inside it.

For Black women specifically: Research by Dr. Cheryl Woods-Giscombé on the Superwoman Schema documents how Black women are often socialized to suppress vulnerability, appear strong at all costs, and avoid reliance on others — responses that can make nervous system dysregulation invisible, even to yourself. You may have spent years not knowing your nervous system was dysregulated because you were too busy keeping everything running. You deserve to know what's been happening in your body.

✍🏾 Reflect

Mapping Your Patterns

These prompts invite curiosity, not judgment. You're a scientist studying your own experience.

Prompt 01

Looking at the four survival responses — fight/flight, freeze, fawn — which one or two feel most familiar to you? When do you tend to go there?

Prompt 02

Think about a moment recently when you felt close to your window of tolerance — not quite flooded, not quite shut down. What helped you stay there? What pulled you out?

Prompt 03

Where in your body do you feel your nervous system "going off"? Circle or write the areas where you notice tension, heaviness, fluttering, or numbness (examples: chest, throat, stomach, shoulders, jaw, hands).

Write or draw the areas in your body where you most often feel stress, tension, or activation.
(Examples: tight throat, heavy chest, clenched stomach, numb hands)
Prompt 04

Thinking about the Superwoman Schema — the pressure to always appear strong and capable — how has this shown up in your life? Has it ever made it harder to recognize or acknowledge what your nervous system needed?

🌿 Ground Yourself

The Butterfly Hug + Breath

This bilateral stimulation technique — used in EMDR and trauma-focused approaches — gently activates both sides of the brain and can help move you back toward your window of tolerance when you're feeling flooded or shut down.

Allow 5–8 minutes. This works best when you're seated comfortably. If bilateral tapping doesn't feel comfortable for your body today, just use the breathing portion.

  1. Set your intention. Take one breath. Say silently or aloud: "I am safe in this moment. My body is allowed to rest."

  2. Cross your arms. Cross your hands over your chest, fingertips resting just below your collarbones — like you're giving yourself a hug. Let your thumbs interlace if that's comfortable.

  3. Begin the tapping. Slowly and rhythmically, alternate tapping each hand — left, right, left, right — at a pace that feels calm. Like a slow heartbeat or the pace of a gentle walk.

  4. Add the breath. Breathe in for four counts. Breathe out for six. Keep tapping. Let your exhale be longer than your inhale — this activates your parasympathetic (rest) system.

  5. Bring in something safe. As you tap and breathe, picture someone or something that feels like safety — a person, a pet, a place, a memory, even a character from a book. Let that image or feeling expand in your chest as you breathe.

  6. Continue for 2–3 minutes. Then let your hands rest. Notice what changed — even slightly. Note it here:

"Your nervous system is not your enemy. It is a loyal protector that has been working too hard, for too long, without rest. You are allowed to give it one."
