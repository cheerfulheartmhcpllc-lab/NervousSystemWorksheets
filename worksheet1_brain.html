Worksheet 01 · Brain & Trauma

What Happened
to Your Brain

Understanding the neuroscience of sexual trauma — not to explain you away, but to help you make sense of what your mind and body have been doing to keep you alive.

Your brain did not betray you. In the middle of something unimaginable, it did what brains do — it fought to keep you here. What you are experiencing now is not weakness or brokenness. It is biology doing exactly what it was built to do.

📚 Let's Learn

Three Parts of Your Brain Working Overtime

When trauma happens, three brain regions are at the center of the storm. Understanding what each one does — and what happens when trauma disrupts it — can help you see your responses as logical outcomes, not character flaws.

🔔

The Amygdala

Your brain's alarm system. It detects threat and activates survival responses — immediately, automatically, and without your permission. After trauma, it becomes hypersensitive, firing in situations that feel dangerous even when they aren't.

📂

The Hippocampus

Your brain's filing clerk. It organizes memories in sequence — before, during, after. During high stress, stress hormones shrink its activity, which is why trauma memories can feel fragmented, non-linear, or like they're happening right now.

🧭

The Prefrontal Cortex

Your rational brain — the part that reasons, makes decisions, and regulates emotion. During trauma, when the alarm goes off, this area goes partially offline. It cannot override the survival response. That is not a failure. That is the system working.

Why this matters for you: Fragmented memories, difficulty explaining "exactly what happened," feeling frozen, or reacting strongly to seemingly small things — all of these make complete neurological sense. Your brain was not broken. It was overwhelmed.

The Stress Hormone Flood

During sexual assault, your body releases a surge of cortisol and adrenaline through what is known as the HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis. This flood is protective — it mobilizes your survival system. But when it goes on too long, or when the threat doesn't feel like it's fully over, cortisol levels stay elevated. This is connected to the exhaustion, memory difficulties, and emotional flooding that many survivors experience long after the assault itself.

Did you know: Tonic immobility — the experience of being unable to move, speak, or fight back during sexual assault — occurs in an estimated 50–75% of survivors. It is an involuntary neurological reflex, not a choice. Not consent. Not passivity. It is your nervous system doing what nervous systems do when escape is not possible.

Why Memories Work Differently After Trauma

Standard memories are encoded in an organized way — you remember where you were, what happened, what came next. Traumatic memories often aren't stored like that. Because the hippocampus is partially overwhelmed, trauma memories can be encoded as fragments: a smell, a sound, a physical sensation, a flash of an image. This is why recounting an assault may feel patchy or non-linear. It is not confusion. It is trauma's neurological fingerprint.

Research by Dr. Bessel van der Kolk and colleagues has shown that traumatic memories are stored differently in the brain — often as sensory fragments rather than narrative sequences. This explains why certain sounds, smells, or physical sensations can trigger vivid emotional or physical re-experiencing without a clear "story" attached.

✍🏾 Reflect

What Have You Noticed?

Take your time with these. There are no right answers. This is just for you.

Prompt 01

When you think about what happened, what does your memory feel like? Does it come in pieces? All at once? Do certain moments feel sharp while others feel blurry? Describe it in whatever way makes sense to you.

Prompt 02

Are there things — a smell, a sound, a place, a time of day — that bring the memory or the feeling back without warning? List anything that comes to mind, without judgment.

Prompt 03

Before learning about the brain's response to trauma, how did you explain your reactions to yourself? What words did you use — about yourself, about what happened? Has anything shifted in how you see it now?

Prompt 04

What is one thing from the psychoeducation section that surprised you, or that you wish someone had told you sooner?

🌿 Ground Yourself

The Orienting Exercise

When your brain is stuck in threat mode, it helps to give it clear, present-tense information: I am here. I am safe right now. This moment is real. The orienting exercise does exactly that — it asks your nervous system to notice the present, slowly, without pressure.

Allow 5–10 minutes. You can do this sitting or standing. Read slowly.

  1. Arrive. Sit somewhere you feel relatively comfortable. Let your feet rest flat on the floor. If that doesn't feel good, find a position that does — there is no wrong way to sit.

  2. Slow your gaze. Rather than looking at one thing, let your eyes wander slowly around the room — like you're seeing it for the first time. Notice colors, textures, shapes. No need to name them. Just let your eyes explore at their own pace.

  3. Name what's here. Silently or out loud, name five things you can see. Not what you feel about them — just what they are. A table. A window. A cup. A crack in the paint. The color of the wall.

  4. Feel the ground. Press your feet gently into the floor. Notice the pressure. Notice that the ground is there, solid and real. You are supported. If your feet don't reach the floor, press your hands flat onto your thighs instead.

  5. One breath. Breathe in slowly for four counts. Hold for two. Breathe out for six. You do not have to do this perfectly. You just have to do it.

  6. Check in. Place one hand over your heart. Without judgment, notice: Where am I on a scale of 1–10 right now? What does my body feel? What does it need?

After the exercise: How do you feel, compared to when you started? You don't have to feel dramatically different. Even a small shift — a little more air in your lungs, a tiny bit less tension in your shoulders — is your nervous system responding. That matters.
