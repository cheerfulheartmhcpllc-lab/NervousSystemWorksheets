Reclaiming
Who You Are

Exploring how sexual assault disrupts your sense of self — and beginning the slow, real, deeply personal work of coming home to yourself again.

What happened to you changed things. But it did not change what you are made of. The parts of you that are curious, that laugh, that love, that believe in something greater than this moment — those are still here. Still yours. This work is about finding your way back to them.

📚 Let's Learn

How Trauma Disrupts Identity

Sexual assault does not just violate a body. It can fracture a person's sense of who they are, who they can trust, and whether they are fundamentally safe in the world. Dr. Judith Herman described trauma as shattering "the constructed system of meaning that gives life its sense of order and coherence." In plain terms: the assault didn't just hurt you in the moment. It may have upended the story you were telling yourself about yourself — and the world.

This is especially true for Black women, who often carry the weight of cultural narratives about strength, composure, and self-sufficiency alongside the internal reality of deep wounds. When your community, your faith tradition, or even your own internal voice says you should be stronger than this, the gap between who you think you "should" be and how you actually feel can become its own source of pain.

Shame vs. Guilt: A Critical Distinction

One of the most damaging things sexual assault does is plant shame. Not guilt — shame. The difference is not semantic. It is clinical, and it matters enormously for healing.

The truth about shame after assault: Shame belongs to the person who chose to assault you — not to you. You did not cause it. You did not invite it. You did not deserve it. And yet the brain, searching for a way to make sense of what happened, often turns shame inward because that feels more controllable than sitting with the reality that someone else chose to harm you. Recognizing that your shame is misplaced is not the same as instantly releasing it. But naming it is the beginning.

The Superwoman Burden

Dr. Cheryl Woods-Giscombé's research on the Superwoman Schema describes how many Black women internalize a set of impossible standards: never show weakness, never ask for help, always put others first, and appear strong even when falling apart inside. Sexual assault does not care about those expectations. The gap between what you're "supposed" to feel and what you actually feel — the shame of vulnerability in a context where vulnerability was violently used against you — is its own distinct wound.

Healing permission slip: You are allowed to need help. You are allowed to not be okay. You are allowed to cry, to fall apart, to be a mess, to go slowly. The strength that matters most right now is not the kind that performs composure. It's the kind that shows up for therapy, does this worksheet, and keeps choosing — even on hard days — to come back to yourself.

Betrayal Trauma and Trust

When sexual assault is committed by someone known — a partner, a family member, a mentor, a colleague — it produces what researcher Dr. Jennifer Freyd called betrayal trauma. The violation isn't only of your body; it is of your trust, your sense of safety in relationship, and sometimes your entire social world. Betrayal trauma carries unique challenges: you may feel unable to trust your own perception of people, doubt your judgment, or feel profoundly alone in a way that goes beyond the assault itself.

Your judgment is not broken. It was manipulated by someone who knew how to exploit trust. That is a reflection of their character, not yours. The fact that you trusted a person who harmed you does not make you naive — it makes you someone who was deceived. Those are not the same thing.

Your Roots: What the Trauma Cannot Take

No matter what the assault disrupted, there are parts of you that preceded it and that persist through it. Your values. The things that make you laugh. What moves you. Who you show up for. What you believe in. These are your roots — and they are worth recovering and naming.

Rewriting the Narrative

Trauma creates a story. Healing asks you to examine that story — not to erase what happened, but to see it more fully and more truthfully.

Prompt 01

What has the assault led you to believe about yourself? List the messages — even the ones that feel ugly or painful to write. You don't have to believe them to write them. Getting them out of your head and onto paper is part of the work.

Prompt 02 — Narrative Reframe

For each painful belief you wrote above, try writing a more truthful counter-statement — not toxic positivity, not a forced affirmation, but a statement grounded in reality. (Example: Shame narrative: "I should have stopped it." Truthful counter: "I responded the way my nervous system was built to respond under threat.")

Prompt 03

Who are you outside of what happened to you? Not who you wish you were, or who you used to be — but who you are, right now, beneath the pain. What are two or three things you know are still true about you?

Prompt 04

If a younger version of you — before all of this — could see you right now, doing this work, showing up for yourself in the middle of pain: what do you think she would say?

🌿 Ground Yourself

A Letter to Yourself

Self-compassion research by Dr. Kristin Neff demonstrates that treating yourself with the same warmth you would offer a close friend who was suffering is one of the most evidence-based practices for healing shame and rebuilding self-worth. This exercise asks you to try that — not perfectly, but honestly.

  1. Think of someone you love. A friend, a sibling, a younger family member — someone you would fiercely defend and gently comfort if they came to you in pain.

  2. Imagine they went through what you went through. Hold that in your mind. What would you say to them? What would they need to hear from you?

  3. Now write that letter to yourself. Use the space below. Begin with: "Dear ___," and write what you would say to her — to yourself. You can write about what happened, about how hard it's been, about what you wish she knew. Let it be imperfect. Let it be real.

Dear ___,
With love, _____

A word about faith: If you have a spiritual practice, this is a moment to bring it in. Many survivors find it meaningful to incorporate prayer, sacred text, or a sense of divine presence into self-compassion work — as a reminder that their worth is not contingent on what was done to them, but on who they inherently are. You are allowed to let that anchor you here.

✨ Take These With You

Truths Worth Returning To

These are not empty phrases. They are starting points. Read them slowly. Circle the one that lands hardest today.

What happened to you is not the sum of who you are.
Your worth was not given to you by other people, and it cannot be taken by them either.
Healing is not linear. There is no timeline you are failing to meet.
You survived something no one should survive alone. You are not required to carry this alone anymore.
Your body is still yours. Your story is still yours. Your future is still yours.
Softness is not weakness. Asking for help is not weakness. Feeling things is not weakness.
"You are not a survivor despite your wounds. You are a whole person — with wounds, with gifts, with grief, with beauty — and all of that belongs to you."
Thank you for doing this work. Thank you for showing up for yourself today. That took something.
